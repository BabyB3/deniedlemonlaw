How to appeal a denied lemon law claim in California

A manufacturer's denial is its opinion — not a final verdict. In California you generally have several ways to push back, and many denials are reconsidered once the records tell the story clearly.

The short answer: You can respond to the manufacturer with stronger documentation, pursue arbitration, or bring a claim under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act — which lets a prevailing buyer recover attorney's fees, so many drivers can pursue a claim at little upfront cost.

Your options after a denial

  1. Respond to the manufacturer directly. Identify the exact reason for the denial and answer it with organized records — a grouped timeline of repeat visits, the customer-complaint lines, and any evidence the technician couldn't capture. Many "first-pass" denials don't survive a clear, documented rebuttal.
  2. Pursue arbitration. Many manufacturers participate in a dispute-resolution program such as BBB AUTO LINE, and California has a state Arbitration Certification Program. Arbitration is generally faster and less formal than court, though the rules and effects vary by program.
  3. Bring a claim under the Song-Beverly Act. If the manufacturer won't resolve it, the law itself provides a path. Because of the fee-shifting rule below, this is more accessible than many people assume.

The fee-shifting rule that changes the math

California's lemon law includes a powerful provision: if the buyer prevails, the manufacturer can be required to pay the buyer's reasonable attorney's fees and costs. In practice, that's why many lemon law attorneys take qualifying cases on contingency — meaning little or no upfront cost to you. A denial that felt like the end of the road often isn't, once you understand this.

Build the appeal around the exact denial reason

The strongest appeals respond precisely to why the claim was denied. Start with the stated reason in your denial letter, then use the matching guide:

  • "Not enough repair attempts" — recount by grouping symptoms and days out of service.
  • "Could not duplicate" — lean on the complaint line and repeat-visit pattern.
  • "Too many miles" — anchor the first-reported date inside the warranty.
  • "Not covered under warranty" — challenge a misapplied "wear" label.
  • "Modifications" — demand causation evidence.

Don't wait too long

Time limits apply to lemon law and warranty claims, and a denial doesn't pause them. The specifics depend on your situation, so it's generally wise to organize your records and weigh your options promptly rather than letting time pass.

Frequently asked questions

Can you appeal a denied lemon law claim in California?

Yes. A denial is the manufacturer's position, not a final ruling. You can respond with stronger documentation, pursue arbitration, or bring a Song-Beverly claim. Many denials are reconsidered once the repair history is organized and the exact reason is addressed.

Does it cost money to pursue a claim?

California's lemon law lets a prevailing buyer recover reasonable attorney's fees and costs, so many attorneys take qualifying cases on contingency — little or no upfront cost. This is general information, not a guarantee about any case.

Is there a deadline to act?

Yes — time limits apply and are situation-specific, and a denial doesn't pause them. It's generally wise not to wait.
