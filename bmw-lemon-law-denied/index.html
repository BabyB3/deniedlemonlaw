BMW lemon law claim denied in California?

A BMW denial isn't always final. California's lemon law covers BMW just like any automaker — and the electrical and engine complaints BMW owners report are often the kind that get dismissed as "normal characteristics" but turn out to be documented defects.

The short answer: BMW vehicles under a manufacturer's warranty are covered by California's Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. A defect labeled a "normal characteristic" of a performance car is not automatically excluded — what it does to your vehicle's safety, value, and use is what matters.

How California's lemon law applies to BMW

Every BMW sold or leased with a manufacturer's warranty falls under California's lemon law. The standard: a warranty-covered defect that substantially impairs use, value, or safety, which BMW can't repair after a reasonable number of attempts. The presumption can apply if, within 18 months or 18,000 miles, there were 4+ attempts for the same defect, 2+ for a serious safety defect, or 30+ cumulative days out of service — across the 3 Series, 5 Series, X3, X5, and the rest of the lineup.

BMW complaints that often lead to denials

BMWs are electronics- and engine-complex, and dealers sometimes explain recurring issues away as inherent to a performance vehicle. Commonly reported categories include:

  • Electrical and iDrive electronics — warning messages, infotainment faults, and sensor errors.
  • Oil and coolant leaks — gasket and seal issues that recur after repair.
  • Cooling-system and engine concerns — overheating, power loss, and drivetrain warnings.
  • Transmission and drivetrain faults — harsh shifts and intermittent driveline issues.

Your claim turns on your vehicle's own documented history, not the brand's reputation. Look up your exact year and model's recalls and complaints (the records check on the homepage pulls this from the federal NHTSA database), and note any technical service bulletins your dealer referenced.

If BMW denied your claim

  1. Pin the exact denial reason — "normal characteristic," "could not duplicate," "not enough attempts," or "out of warranty."
  2. Challenge a "characteristic" label by tying the defect to safety, drivability, or value.
  3. Collect every repair order for the issue and group repeat visits together.
  4. Note acknowledgments — service bulletins or goodwill repairs can show the issue is a known defect.

Two common BMW denials have dedicated guides: "could not duplicate" and repairs "not covered under warranty."

Frequently asked questions

Can you file a lemon law claim against BMW in California?

Yes. BMW vehicles under a manufacturer's warranty are covered by California's Song-Beverly Act — for a substantial, warranty-covered defect BMW can't fix after a reasonable number of attempts.

BMW called it a "normal characteristic" — valid?

Not always. Whether that label holds depends on the defect's impact. A problem that affects safety, drivability, or value — or that BMW acknowledged in a service bulletin — is often more than a characteristic.

What are common BMW problems behind these claims?

Reported categories include electrical/iDrive electronics, oil and coolant leaks, cooling/engine concerns, and drivetrain faults. Your vehicle's documented history is what counts — check it against NHTSA.
